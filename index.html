Holiday scams: a Pittsford guide to staying safe

The holiday season means busy schedules, online shopping, travel, year-end donations, and packages on every porch. Scammers know this and they target people in communities like Pittsford with fake websites, “urgent” texts, and calls that try to rush you into paying or sharing information.

This page walks through the most common holiday scams and what you can do right away if something feels wrong.

1. “Too good to be true” online holiday deals

Scammers set up fake online stores and social media ads that look very close to real brands. Around the holidays they push “limited time” deals on toys, electronics, or hot items that are in short supply. Often you never receive the item, or your card is used for other charges.

Scenario
The perfect gift that never arrives

A Pittsford parent sees an ad on social media for a game console that is sold out everywhere else. The site looks polished, the price is far lower than other stores, and the ad says the sale ends tonight. They pay with a card and get a confirmation email, but the tracking number never works. A week later there are extra charges on the card from unrelated merchants.

Signs it might be a fake store

  • Prices are much lower than other trusted retailers for the exact same item.
  • The web address is slightly off: extra letters, odd spelling, or strange endings.
  • There is no clear physical address or customer service phone number on the site.
  • The seller insists on payment through apps, wire transfer, crypto, or gift cards.
Tip: Type the store name into a search engine with words like “review” or “scam” before you buy. If there are complaints, choose another seller.

2. Travel and holiday rental scams

Winter break, ski weekends, and family trips are prime time for fake rental listings. Scammers copy real listings or invent properties that do not exist, then push you to pay a deposit in unusual ways or move the conversation off the main platform.

Scenario
The “perfect” cabin outside town

A couple from Monroe County finds a “last minute deal” on a cabin for New Year’s Eve. The photos look amazing and the price is far below similar places. The “owner” asks them to text directly instead of using the booking site and wants a deposit by gift card or payment app. When they arrive, the cabin is already occupied by the real renters, and the host they paid has vanished.

Red flags for rental scams

  • The host will not let you book and pay through a trusted platform.
  • You are asked to pay with gift cards, wire transfer, crypto, or a payment app to a stranger.
  • Rent is far below similar properties in the same area.
  • The host pressures you to “decide right now” or you will lose the deal.
Safer approach: Book through trusted platforms that handle payment, keep messages inside the app, and show real reviews.

3. Gift card and “family emergency” scams

One of the biggest holiday scam patterns is simple: someone contacts you with a scary or emotional story and asks you to pay using gift cards. Federal consumer protection agencies are very clear on this point. Gift cards are for gifts, not for payments.

Scenario
“Grandma, I need help right now”

A Pittsford grandparent gets a call that sounds like a grandchild in trouble. The caller claims there has been an accident and says the only way to fix the problem is to buy several gift cards and read the codes on the back over the phone. The caller begs them not to tell anyone else. After the cards are drained, they discover the real grandchild is fine. It was a scammer using an imitation voice.

How these scams usually work

  • Scammers pretend to be a family member, government agency, utility, bank, or tech support.
  • They create urgency: “today only”, “you will be arrested”, “your loved one is in danger”.
  • They tell you exactly which gift cards to buy and where to buy them.
  • They ask for the numbers and codes on the back of the cards over the phone or in a message.
Hard rule: If someone asks you to pay with gift cards, it is a scam. Hang up or stop texting, and talk to someone you trust before you do anything else.

4. Package delivery texts and “tracking” links

With more packages on porches, scammers send fake texts and emails that pretend to be from major delivery companies or the postal service. Some link to sites that steal card numbers, others try to install malware, and some collect personal data they can use later.

Scenario
“We could not deliver your package”

A resident gets a text saying a package is held due to an unpaid delivery fee. The link looks similar to a real carrier’s site but asks for a full card number, date of birth, and Social Security number. After entering the details, they start seeing new charges on their card and strange activity on other accounts.

Safer ways to handle delivery messages

  • Do not tap suspicious links in texts or emails.
  • Go directly to the delivery company website by typing the address yourself.
  • Use tracking numbers from your original order confirmation only.
  • If a message asks for personal details or payment for a package you do not recognize, ignore it.

5. What to do if something feels off

Trust your instincts. Feeling rushed, pressured, or a little confused is often the first sign that a message or call is not normal. You can pause and check things without being rude.

Quick steps if you are not sure

  1. End the call or ignore the message. You can always call back using a trusted number.
  2. Do not click links or open attachments until you are sure they are real.
  3. Contact the company using a phone number or website you look up yourself.
  4. Talk to a friend, family member, or neighbor before you send money or information.
You are allowed to slow things down. Scammers rely on panic and speed. Real businesses and real family emergencies can handle a short delay while you confirm details.

6. If you already paid or shared information

If you think you have been scammed, take action quickly. Even if you cannot get all of your money back, reporting helps protect others.

If you gave a credit or debit card number

  1. Call the number on the back of your card and explain what happened.
  2. Ask the bank to review recent charges and cancel the card if needed.
  3. Watch your statements and enable alerts for new charges.

If you paid with gift cards

  1. Contact the company that issued the gift card right away and tell them it was used in a scam.
  2. Keep the receipt and card if you still have them.
  3. Report the scam to the Federal Trade Commission at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.

If personal information was exposed

  1. Change passwords on your email and financial accounts and turn on multi-factor login.
  2. Consider placing a fraud alert or credit freeze by contacting the major credit bureaus.
  3. Watch for new accounts or loans you did not open.
