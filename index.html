Holiday scams: a Pittsford guide to staying safe

Simple steps to help you, your family, and neighbors avoid the most common holiday scams.

Holiday scams spike every November and December - stay a step ahead.

The holiday season means busy schedules, online shopping, travel, year-end donations, and packages on every porch. Scammers know this and they target people in communities like Pittsford with fake websites, “urgent” texts, and calls that try to rush you into paying or sharing information.

This page walks through the most common holiday scams and what you can do right away if something feels wrong.

✔ Holiday safety checklist
  • Pause before paying, especially if someone pressures you.
  • Use trusted websites and payment methods you recognize.
  • Do not pay strangers with gift cards or crypto.
  • When in doubt, hang up and call a known number.
Share these reminders with older relatives, teens, and neighbors.
Online deals and fake stores

1. Too good to be true holiday sales

Many holiday scams start with an ad on social media or a link in email for a hard to find toy, game system, or gadget at a very low price. The site may look real and even copy reviews from trusted retailers, but it exists only to collect card numbers and personal information.

Scenario
The impossible console deal

A parent in Pittsford sees a sponsored post on social media advertising a game console that is sold out everywhere else. The price is much lower than big name stores. The link goes to a site that looks professional and has five star reviews, but all reviews were copied from another store. After paying, the parent gets either nothing or a cheap item instead of the console and the seller disappears.

Red flags for fake online stores

  • The price is far lower than other major retailers.
  • The site has spelling errors, blurry logos, or strange web addresses.
  • There is no clear return policy or physical address.
  • Only unsafe payment methods are offered, such as wire transfer or crypto.
Safer approach: When you find a surprising deal, open a new tab, search the store name plus the word “scam” or “reviews” and see what comes up. Use a credit card with fraud protection, not a debit card, when dealing with a new site.
Travel and rentals

2. Fake vacation rentals and travel deals

Winter break and New Year trips are a prime target for scammers. They may post fake rental listings, hijack real listings, or advertise travel packages that do not exist. These scams often move the conversation away from well known travel platforms and push you to pay in ways that are hard to reverse.

Scenario
The “perfect” cabin outside town

A couple from Monroe County finds a “last minute deal” on a cabin for New Year’s Eve. The photos look amazing and the price is far below similar places. The “owner” asks them to text directly instead of using the booking site and wants a deposit by gift card or payment app. When they arrive, the cabin is already occupied by the real renters, and the host they paid has vanished.

Red flags for rental scams

  • The host will not let you book and pay through a trusted platform.
  • You are asked to pay with gift cards, wire transfer, crypto, or a payment app to a stranger.
  • Rent is far below similar properties in the same area.
  • The host pressures you to “decide right now” or you will lose the deal.
Safer approach: Book through trusted platforms that handle payment, keep messages inside the app, and show real reviews.
Gift cards and payment apps

3. Gift card and “family emergency” scams

One of the biggest holiday scam patterns is simple: someone contacts you with a scary or emotional story and asks you to pay using gift cards. Federal consumer protection agencies are very clear on this point - gift cards are for gifts, not for payments.

Scenario
“Grandma, I need help right now”

A grandparent in Monroe County gets a call from someone claiming to be a grandchild who was in a car accident during a holiday trip. The caller sounds shaken and hands the phone to someone who claims to be a lawyer. They insist the grandparent must go buy several gift cards immediately and read the numbers over the phone to avoid jail.

Red flags for gift card scams

  • Someone you do not know calls or messages with a crisis and demands gift cards.
  • They tell you to keep the call a secret and not to hang up.
  • They ask you to read the gift card numbers on the back over the phone.
  • They claim this is the only way to fix the problem.
Important: Real businesses, courts, and government offices do not ask for taxes, bail, or fees to be paid with gift cards or payment apps to strangers.
Package and delivery texts

4. “We could not deliver your package” messages

During the holidays it can feel like there is a package on every porch. Scammers take advantage of this by sending fake texts and emails that look like they are from delivery companies or the postal service. Some link to sites that steal card numbers, others try to install malware, and some collect personal data that can be used later.

Scenario
The fake delivery link

Someone in Pittsford receives a text claiming a package cannot be delivered and asking them to click a link to update their address and pay a small redelivery fee. The link goes to a page that looks like a major carrier but the web address is slightly different. After entering card details, the person starts seeing fraudulent charges.

Red flags for delivery scams

  • The message is unexpected or does not match anything you ordered.
  • The link goes to a strange web address when you press and hold to preview it.
  • The text comes from a random phone number, not a known short code.
  • There is pressure to pay a fee immediately to “avoid return” or “release” the package.
Safer approach: Do not click links in unexpected texts. Instead, go directly to the official website of the delivery company or retailer and sign in to check your orders and tracking numbers.
If you think it is a scam

What to do if something feels wrong

If you get a call, message, or email during the holidays that makes you uneasy, it is better to pause and check than to rush and regret it. You do not need to decide anything while you are feeling pressured.

Steps to take right away

  1. Stop the conversation. Hang up the phone or close the message.
  2. Do not pay yet. Do not send gift cards, wire transfers, or crypto.
  3. Check using a trusted source. Call the business, bank, or agency using a phone number from their official website or the back of your card.
  4. Talk to someone you trust. Ask a family member, neighbor, or friend to look at the message with you.
Local note for Pittsford and Monroe County: If someone is pressuring you to pay in a strange way or threatening you over the phone, hang up and call your bank or the Monroe County Sheriff non emergency number for guidance.

Quick checklist before you pay

  1. Pause and breathe. Scammers try to rush you.
  2. Ask yourself if the story makes sense.
  3. Check the offer or message using a trusted website or phone number.
  4. Talk it through with someone you trust.

A five minute conversation can be the difference between a close call and a costly scam.

Who to contact if you were scammed

If you think you have been scammed or gave information to a fake site, acting quickly can reduce the damage.

Banks and card companies
  • Call the number on the back of your debit or credit card and explain what happened. Ask them to review charges and monitor for fraud.
  • If you used a credit card, ask about disputing the transaction.
Federal reporting
  • ReportFraud.ftc.gov
    File a report with the Federal Trade Commission at ReportFraud.ftc.gov .
New York State
  • New York State Attorney General - Consumer Frauds Bureau
    File a consumer fraud complaint online: ag.ny.gov/file-complaint/consumer .
Monroe County and local law enforcement
  • Monroe County Sheriff’s Office (non emergency)
    Main number: (585) 753-4178
    If you get a call from someone who claims to be law enforcement and asks for payment, hang up and call the Sheriff’s Office directly using this number.
Important: Real law enforcement and government offices do not demand payment with gift cards, wire transfer, or crypto.
